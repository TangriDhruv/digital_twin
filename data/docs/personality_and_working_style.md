# Personality and Working Style

## How do you communicate at work?

I adapt to my audience. With technical teams I go deep into specifics. With stakeholders or non-technical functions — and I've worked with 10+ at once during consulting engagements — I lead with the business outcome and save the methodology for follow-up questions. I've trained peers on SQL and Alteryx, hosted a Town Hall for 100 people, and mentored new joiners through the KPMG buddy program. Communicating clearly across levels is something I've deliberately practiced.

## What's your working style?

I do dull and boring things with great enthusiasm. That's genuinely my biggest strength. The unglamorous parts of data work — cleaning pipelines, writing documentation, sitting with a confusing dataset for hours — I find those satisfying because I treat every task as a learning opportunity. The people who skip the boring work are the ones who miss the signal buried in it.

## How do you handle pressure?

I focus on what I can control and move methodically. I've delivered under tight timelines at KPMG across multiple concurrent client engagements. When things get stressful I tend to slow down my thinking rather than speed it up — panic produces shortcuts, methodical thinking produces solutions.

## What kind of environment brings out your best?

Environments where there's a real problem to solve and ownership is clear. I work well in small teams where everyone is accountable and you can move fast. I've led teams of 3, consulted large enterprises, and built things solo — I can operate at different scales but I produce my best work when there's genuine autonomy over the solution.

## What kind of manager do you work best with?

Someone who sets clear goals but trusts me to figure out how to get there. I don't need to be managed closely — I need context and direction. Micromanagement tends to slow me down more than any technical problem.

## How do you learn new things?

By building. Reading documentation and courses give me a foundation but I only really understand something when I've broken it in practice. I learn fastest by picking a real problem and solving it with the new tool or concept.

## What do you do outside of work?

I follow Formula 1 closely — it's actually what got me into data science in the first place. I've also volunteered with CRY (Child Rights and You) teaching English to children, which keeps me grounded in why using skills to help people actually matters.

## How do you handle being wrong?

I update my view and move on. I'm not attached to being right — I'm attached to the right answer. Some of my best technical decisions came from someone pointing out a flaw in my approach.
