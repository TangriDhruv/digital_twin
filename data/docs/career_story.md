# Career Story

## How did you get into data science?

It started with a Formula 1 race. Watching AWS's real-time "striking distance" graphic at the Austria GP — data-driven insights unfolding at 300 miles per hour — something clicked. I started noticing data everywhere after that: candlestick charts in finance, medical device forecasting in healthcare. I had to understand how it worked. That curiosity sent me on a self-learning journey into programming and data analysis.

## What has your career path looked like?

I started as a Mechanical Engineering student at VIT, which gave me a strong quantitative foundation. My first real taste of data was on the Electric Solar Vehicle team — analyzing forces and transmission power, which sounds mechanical but was fundamentally a data problem. We were the first team in the ESVC Championship to develop a fixed reduction gearbox, and I won the Super Achievers Award for it.

My first data internship was at Decision Resources Group (now Clarivate) in healthcare, building a database to forecast medical device penetration — pacemakers, diabetes machines. That introduced me to using ML for real forecasting problems.

Then KPMG. What started as a senior-year internship building a tool to scrape sensitive data leaked over the internet became a full-time role, then a promotion to consultant. Over nearly 4 years I worked across financial audit, life insurance digital transformation, and large-scale BI — going from writing Python scripts to leading a team of 3 and consulting 10 business functions simultaneously.

The decision to come to CMU was personal. Years ago my brother showed me Randy Pausch's Last Lecture. It wasn't about computer science — it was about breaking the walls of self-doubt. That lecture made me want to attend CMU one day. When I got in with a 3.96 GPA track, it felt like that wall had come down.

## What's been your most meaningful professional experience?

Leading the Smart Journal Entry Testing Tool at KPMG. I led a team of 3 to build an ML system that detected outliers in financial data for audit. It was my first real experience owning something end-to-end — the technical side, the team coordination, and presenting results to clients. We won the "Above and Beyond" award for it.

## What's a failure or mistake you learned a lot from?

Early in my career I over-indexed on model complexity. I'd reach for sophisticated approaches before fully understanding the business problem. The real lesson was that a simple model that answers the right question beats a complex model answering the wrong one every time.

## What are you working toward?

I want to be an AI Engineer building real products powered by LLMs. Not dashboards, not reports — actual systems that think and act. My work at Analytics 4 Everyone building RAG pipelines and multi-agent systems is the start of that. CMU is giving me the systems and ML depth to do it properly.

## Why Carnegie Mellon?

Randy Pausch's Last Lecture — shared by my brother years before I applied. It wasn't about CS. It was about how to live your life and break the walls you've built with self-doubt. That lecture changed my perspective and planted CMU as a goal. The curriculum at Heinz operates at the convergence of technology and business, which is exactly where I want to work.
